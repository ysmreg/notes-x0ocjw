Why Everyone is Buying the 100th Anniversary Rolex Oyster Right Now

The original Oyster case changed watchmaking forever in 1926. One hundred years later, Rolex marked the centennial with a collection that manages to honor the original proportions while packing every modern advancement the brand has developed since. The secondary market noticed immediately.

What Rolex actually released for the centennial

Three new references dropped at Watches and Wonders 2026, each featuring the heritage-style flat bezel and recessed crown guard that recall the 1926 patent drawings. The cases are 36mm Oystersteel with a satin-polished finish Rolex is calling Ice Grain — a micro-textured surface that catches light differently from anything in the current catalog. Inside sits the calibre 3230, COSC-certified and Superlative Chronometer rated to plus-or-minus two seconds per day.

Why collectors are paying over retail

The grey market premium hit forty percent within seventy-two hours of the announcement. Three factors are driving that: limited first-year production confirmed by Rolex communications director Arnaud Boetsch, the centennial provenance that gives every unit a built-in narrative for future auction catalogs, and the fact that Rolex has never repeated an anniversary edition — the 50th Submariner and the 50th Daytona both appreciated sharply after their initial runs ended.

The historical significance most buyers miss

Hans Wilsdorf filed the Oyster patent on 24 November 1926. The case was not just water-resistant — it was the first wristwatch case that could be certified by an independent body for dust and moisture protection. That single innovation turned the wristwatch from a fragile accessory into a tool. Every sports watch made since owes something to that patent.

The Oyster case did not just protect the movement. It gave people permission to wear a mechanical watch outside a drawing room for the first time.

Should you buy now or wait

Waiting for retail allocation can stretch twelve to eighteen months at most authorized dealers. The grey market premium is steep but predictable — Rolex anniversary pieces have never traded below retail after their production window closed. Collectors with access to an AD relationship and purchase history are in the strongest position. Everyone else faces a simple bet: pay forty percent over retail now, or risk paying sixty percent over when first-year production numbers become public and confirm scarcity.
